April 23, 2003

Taiwan copyright laws seen as unreasonable

Taiwan News, Staff Reporter / By Lisa Wang

Student groups yesterday voiced strong opposition to the ongoing revision of the country's copyright laws, saying that some of the major amendments were unreasonable and that the government was simply acting under pressure from the United States.

Legislators are scheduled to review amendments to the copyright law today, ahead of the release by a local U.S. office of an annual special 301 watch list later this month.

"The government is under pressure from the U.S. to improve the nation's IPR environment," said Hsu Pei-ran (許沛然), spokesman for a student alliance against the revision. "Taiwan is making too many concessions on the issue."

"The revision comes at a sensitive time when the Office of the U.S. Trade Representative is set to release the annual 301 watch list," he added.

An official from the Intellectual Property Office (智財局) admitted that the IPR issue had been used as a bargaining chip by U.S. representatives in trade negotiations between the two nations.

The student alliance is comprised of dozens of cultural and environmental groups from Taiwan's colleges, including College Forum of National Taiwan University and Focus on Globalization.

"The proposed revisions, which include conditions for wider public prosecution, are too strict and are unacceptable to Taiwanese people," Hsu said.

Some powerful U.S. interest groups, such as International Intellectual Property Alliance, have long urged that as part of efforts to curb rampant IPR violations, persons who breach the copyright law should be subject to public prosecution, Hsu said.

But he further explained that World Trade Organization member countries are not required to bring copyright law violations under the umbrella of public prosecution.

"The law in Taiwan is not that strict as it was modeled on similar legislation in most European countries. Once students do not distribute or sell pirated CDs, they have no need to worry about the law," said Robin Lee (李瑞斌), CEO of Taiwan's International Federation of the Phonographic Industry unit, yesterday.

"The revision does not purport to service the U.S. Most members of the IFPI are local record companies," said Lee.

Taiwan's record industry has sustained about 20 percent loss in sales every year since 1997, according to the IFPI official. Local music companies recorded less than NT$5 billion in sales in 2002, compared to NT$12 billion in 1997.

"The rule will hinder the circulation of information and knowledge as most students cannot afford to buy original material. It is quite common on campuses for students to use photocopied text books and CDs burned by classmates," said Hsu, who is currently a journalism major at National Chengchi University.

Multinational record companies and large mainstream music studios, rather than music producers or underground musicians, will be the biggest beneficiaries of the revised law, he told the Taiwan News.

The alliance is set to stage a protest in front of Ministry of Justice today.
